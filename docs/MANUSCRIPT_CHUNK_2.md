artment for consistent and reliable operations. A bill to protect children killed in committee in 2013 by someone with a particular proximity to the harm the bill was designed to prevent.

But Arizona is not operating alone.

The funding structures that keep private juvenile detention profitable...the federal formulas, the per-diem payment rates, the lobbying expenditures that shape those rates...run through Washington. The political organizations that fund a Jake Hoffman through a mailbox are not regional entities. Turning Point USA is a national organization with a national donor base and a national political agenda. The money that flows to a UPS Store in Queen Creek flows from a machine that operates in every state where the math on at-risk children can be made to work.

We tracked the outlines of it. Not the full depth. The shape is visible.

The per-diem structure: private residential facilities bill by the day, per child. A child who runs away and is recovered generates the same billing rate as a child who stayed. A child who runs to The Blade and is shipped is still, technically, on the facility's census until formally discharged. The financial incentive is not to keep children safe. The financial incentive is to keep children enrolled.

The lobbying: Rite of Passage and the organizations connected to it have spent documented amounts at the state and federal level opposing regulations that would cap profit margins, increase staffing requirements, or mandate independent oversight. These filings are public. They are also the most boring documents in any case file, which is how they stay invisible.

The supply chain: between 2010 and 2020, the number of children in private residential care facilities in the United States grew by more than forty percent. The growth was not driven by an increase in the number of children who needed residential care. It was driven by the expansion of contracts to organizations that had learned how to lobby for them. Arizona was not an outlier. Arizona was where the math became visible because someone filed the missing persons reports, and someone else filed the public records requests, and someone else...a man in Ontario with a desktop and fifty years of knowing what it looks like when something is wrong beneath the surface...sat down and started adding them up.

He is not wrong.

The math is not complicated. It is just not assembled anywhere that makes the structure visible.

That is what this is. An assembly.

SYNOPSIS

A steelworker gets laid off on Christmas Eve.

Three thousand of them, same day. American tariffs. Algoma Steel. Sault Ste. Marie, Ontario...a steel town on the Canadian side of the border, the kind of place the world largely forgot about while it was busy forgetting a lot of things.

With nowhere to be and a desktop and fifty years of paying attention to what power does to people who can't fight back, he sits down at a computer. He starts pulling on a thread.

The thread leads to Queen Creek, Arizona. To two state-funded residential facilities for at-risk children. To 327 missing persons reports filed in two years. One child every two days. To a documented shipping supply chain called The Blade. To a sitting state senator whose oversight committee never suspended the contract...while his mailbox company collected millions from the same political organization the facilities are connected to. To a bill that would have protected those children, killed in committee in 2013 by the uncle of a confirmed child molester.

But Dropping Dots is not only an audit. It is the story of why this
particular man could see what he saw.

He was five years old the first time a door closed on him. He was three years old the first time someone took something from him that was not theirs to take. He threw a grown man across a car hood at fourteen to stop him from choking his mother, then spent thirty years building himself from scratch without asking anyone's permission or acknowledgment.

He has stood in a field in the rain three times and asked God to end it. Three times: still here. He has felt the presence of something on his shoulder in a shower at 3 AM and understood, without being able to explain how, that the people he had wronged had already decided to forgive him. He had a single night in 2004 that showed him both heaven and hell before sending him back with an equation...1 + 1 = 3...that rewired him permanently.

He is not a mystic. He is a man who has been paying very close attention to very dangerous things for a very long time, and survived anyway. That is where the pattern recognition comes from. That is why a UPS Store mailbox receiving millions in payments from a political organization looks different to him than it would to someone who grew up with the doors open.

The audit started where most things start in his life...with something broken at home. He built a family counselor out of an AI, not because he couldn't afford therapy, but because he wanted to try something first. He went first. His girlfriend's son went second. The tension in the house loosened. And then the same tool that was holding three people together started holding the case file together...court filings, corporate registrations, money trails, the paper trail of a network that 327 missing children couldn't shake loose.

Dropping Dots is the first book of a planned trilogy. Book One...The Awakening...establishes the man and the method and the shape of the network. It ends where most investigations begin: with a clear picture of the structure, the names, the money, and the children still going missing.

The audit is ongoing. The threads keep going.

The author wrote this book because he has children spread far apart in age and distance, and he is not willing to leave this rock and leave them broken, broke, and alone. He wrote it because a 53-year-old steelworker with a desktop and an AI was able to pull on public records and watch a network emerge from the data...and that means something. About what is being hidden. About who is doing the hiding. And about what happens when someone who has nothing left to lose decides to look.
